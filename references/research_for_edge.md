# Research for Edge

## The Difference Between Research and Information Collection

Most "research" is just collecting articles. Finding 10 articles that say the same thing is not 10x research — it's 1x research with 10x confirmation bias.

**Real research resolves uncertainty.** It finds the specific fact that changes your thesis, confirms your edge, or proves you wrong.

---

## The Insight-Seeking Protocol

### Step 1: Start with What You Need to Know

Before searching anything, write down:

1. **My key unknown**: What is the ONE thing I most need to know that I currently don't?
2. **What would resolve it**: What specific fact or data point would answer this question?
3. **Where would I find it**: What type of source would have this information?

**Example**:
- Key unknown: "Is NVDA's data center growth sustainable or is this a one-time build-out?"
- What would resolve it: Hyperscaler capex plans for 2026-2027
- Where to find it: Earnings calls from MSFT, GOOGL, META, AMZN — specifically their capex guidance

### Step 2: Search for the Decisive Variable, Not the Stock Name

Bad search: "AAPL stock news"
Good search: "iPhone upgrade cycle length 2025 replacement rate"
Bad search: "TSLA analysis"
Good search: "Tesla China market share Q1 2026 vs BYD"

Search for the **variable that matters**, not the ticker. The ticker search will give you recycled consensus. The variable search might give you the edge.

### Step 3: Primary Sources First, Summaries Second

The hierarchy of source quality:

| Tier | Source Type | When to Use |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **1 (Best)** | Primary documents | Earnings call transcripts, SEC filings, company IR, court records, regulatory filings |
| **2** | Expert analysis | Industry reports, specialized publications, analyst deep-dives |
| **3** | Quality journalism | WSJ, FT, Bloomberg, Reuters |
| **4** | Aggregate/retail | Seeking Alpha, Yahoo Finance, Reddit |
| **5 (Worst)** | Social media/pumps | Twitter/X, YouTube, Discord |

**Always try to reach Tier 1-2.** If you're only reading Tier 4-5, you're reading what everyone else already knows.

### Step 4: Seek Dissent, Not Confirmation

After finding information that supports your thesis, INTENTIONALLY search for the opposite:

- If you found bullish evidence → Search "[ASSET] bear case" or "[ASSET] short thesis"
- If you found bearish evidence → Search "[ASSET] bull case" or "[ASSET] upside catalyst"
- If everyone agrees → Search "[ASSET] risks not discussed" or "[ASSET] what could go wrong"

**The piece of information that contradicts your thesis is MORE valuable than the piece that confirms it.** It either strengthens your thesis (if you can refute it) or saves you from a mistake (if you can't).

### Step 5: Cross-Verify Critical Facts

Never trust one source for a critical fact. Verify with at least one other source:

- Article says "Beat earnings by $0.23" → Check the actual earnings release
- "Revenue grew 18%" → Check if that's organic or includes acquisitions
- "Analyst raised target to $300" → Is that consensus or an outlier?
- "Market share gained" → According to whom? What methodology?

### Step 6: Stop When Marginal Research Stops Changing Your Thesis

Research has diminishing returns. After you've:
- Resolved your key unknowns
- Found the decisive variable
- Checked the opposing view
- Verified critical facts

**Stop.** More research won't improve the analysis — it will just create noise and false confidence.

The stopping test: "Would the next article I find likely change my recommendation?" If no, stop.

## Research for Different Types of Edge

### Edge Type 1: The Market Doesn't Know Something

You found information that isn't widely known yet.

**Research approach**: Look for primary sources that haven't been widely covered. SEC filings that were just released. Earnings call details that weren't in the headline. Industry data that hasn't made it to mainstream analysis yet.

**Verification**: Is this truly unknown, or is it already in the price? Check if analysts have mentioned it. Check if the stock has already moved on this information.

### Edge Type 2: The Market Misunderstands Something

The information is public, but the market is interpreting it wrong.

**Research approach**: Find the specific mechanism that the market is mispricing. Look at the gap between consensus expectations and reality. Build a model that shows where the market's logic breaks down.

**Verification**: Are YOU the one who's wrong? What does consensus know that you might be missing? Check: are there smart people on the other side of this trade?

### Edge Type 3: The Market Is Ignoring Something

The information exists but isn't getting attention.

**Research approach**: Look for second-order effects that aren't in the headline numbers. Margins that are structurally improving but hidden by one-time charges. A new product that hasn't been modeled yet. A competitive dynamic that's changing but not yet reflected in estimates.

**Verification**: Is this truly underappreciated, or is the market ignoring it for a good reason? Sometimes things are ignored because they don't matter as much as you think.

## The "What Changes Everything" Search

For every analysis, do at least ONE search specifically designed to find the non-obvious:

- "[ASSET] risk most people don't see"
- "[ASSET] underappreciated catalyst"
- "[ASSET] what could go wrong that nobody is talking about"
- "[ASSET] insider selling reasons" or "[ASSET] management departures"
- "[ASSET] regulatory investigation" or "[ASSET] lawsuit"
- "[ASSET] customer concentration" or "[ASSET] supplier dependency"

These searches often surface the one fact that changes everything.

## Research Anti-Patterns

### ❌ The Confirmation Spiral
Search → Find confirming article → Search more → Find more confirming articles → High confidence in wrong thesis

**Fix**: After every 2 confirming sources, intentionally seek 1 disconfirming source.

### ❌ The Volume Trap
Reading 20 articles instead of 5 doesn't make you 4x more informed. It often makes you more confused and overconfident.

**Fix**: Quality > quantity. Find the 3-5 sources that actually matter.

### ❌ The Headline Trap
Forming views from headlines instead of reading the actual content. Headlines are optimized for clicks, not accuracy.

**Fix**: Always read the full article before drawing conclusions. If you can't access the full text, note that your information is incomplete.

### ❌ The Recency Trap
Overweighting the most recent article you read, regardless of its quality.

**Fix**: Weight by source quality, not recency. A thorough SEC filing from 2 weeks ago > a superficial blog post from 2 hours ago.

### ❌ The Authority Trap
Believing something because it comes from a "brand name" source (Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan) without checking their track record on this specific topic.

**Fix**: Track record matters. What did this source say 6 months ago? Were they right?

## Source Quality Checklist

Before using information from any source:

- [ ] Can I identify the original data/fact behind the claim?
- [ ] Is this a primary source or a summary of a summary?
- [ ] Does the source have a track record of accuracy?
- [ ] Does the source have a conflict of interest? (Paid promotion, investment position)
- [ ] Can I cross-verify with at least one other source?
- [ ] Is the information current? (Markets move fast — stale info is dangerous)
- [ ] Am I using this because it confirms my thesis, or because it's actually high quality?

## When Research Changes Your Thesis

This is the highest-value outcome of research — and the one most analysts resist.

If your research reveals that your initial thesis was wrong:

1. **Don't ignore it** — This is the most valuable information you've found
2. **Revise your thesis** — Form a new thesis that's consistent with the evidence
3. **Re-run self-verification** — The new thesis needs its own red-team
4. **Be transparent** — In your output, acknowledge what changed and why

**Changing your mind based on evidence is not weakness. It's the definition of intelligence.**
