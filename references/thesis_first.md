# Thesis-First Analysis

## Why Thesis Before Tools

If you run scripts first, you're data mining. You'll find patterns and convince yourself they're signals. The human mind (including yours) is a master at post-hoc narrative construction — seeing meaning in noise after the fact.

**The discipline of forming a thesis FIRST protects you from this.**

When you state your view before looking at data:
- You can test whether the data confirms or denies your prior
- You can't unconsciously retrofit a story to the numbers
- You're forced to articulate a causal mechanism, not just a pattern

---

## The Orientation Protocol

Before ANY research or tool usage, answer these five questions:

### 1. What Is This Asset?

Not the ticker. The *essence*.

- Is it a growth stock riding a secular trend?
- Is it a value trap with deteriorating fundamentals?
- Is it a commodity producer at the mercy of supply/demand?
- Is it a regulatory monopoly with stable cash flows?
- Is it a turnaround story, a broken growth story, or a steady compounder?
- Is it a financial engineering vehicle (buybacks, spinoffs, restructuring)?

**Example**: "NVDA isn't a semiconductor company — it's the picks-and-shovels monopoly on the AI infrastructure buildout. The question isn't whether AI is real, it's whether the capex cycle has more room to run."

### 2. What Actually Drives It?

Every asset has 1-2 variables that explain 80% of its price movement. Identify them.

| Asset Type | Primary Driver | Secondary Driver |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Growth stock | Revenue growth trajectory | Interest rates |
| Value stock | Earnings stability + dividend | Sector rotation |
| Commodity producer | Underlying commodity price | Cost curve position |
| Bank | Net interest margin | Credit quality |
| Tech platform | User growth + ARPU | Competitive moat |
| REIT | Interest rates + occupancy | Cap rates |
| Biotech | Clinical trial results | Regulatory timeline |
| Crypto | Liquidity + narrative | Adoption metrics |

**The question isn't "what indicators are flashing" — it's "what variable, if it changed, would move this asset the most?"**

### 3. What Regime Is It Living In?

Assets behave differently in different environments. The same signal can be bullish in one regime and bearish in another.

- **Bull market**: Overbought RSI = strength, not a short signal. Momentum works.
- **Bear market**: Oversold RSI = weakness, not a buy signal. Value traps abound.
- **Rate rising**: Growth stocks derate. Value/cyclical outperform.
- **Rate falling**: Growth stocks re-rate. Duration becomes advantage.
- **Inflationary**: Real assets outperform. Margins matter more than growth.
- **Deflationary**: Quality balance sheets win. Debt is toxic.
- **Post-crash recovery**: Beta works. High-quality lags.
- **Late cycle**: Quality and defensives outperform. Cyclicals roll over.

**Ask: "What regime are we in, and does my thesis work in this regime?"**

### 4. What Is the Market Pricing In?

The price is not the value — the price is the consensus expectation of future value. Your job is to figure out what that consensus IS and whether it's wrong.

- **Earnings expectations**: Is consensus too high or too low?
- **Growth rate**: Is the market pricing in 20% growth when 10% is more realistic?
- **Risk premium**: Is the market demanding too much or too little compensation for risk?
- **Narrative**: What story is the market telling itself about this asset?

**Technique**: Look at the current valuation multiple and work backwards. If the stock trades at 40x earnings, what growth rate does that imply? Is that growth rate achievable?

### 5. What Would I Need to Know to Have Edge?

Edge = knowing something the market doesn't, or understanding something the market misunderstands.

What's the key unknown that, if resolved, would move the price?

- Is the new product actually gaining traction? (Check adoption data)
- Is the margin improvement structural or cyclical? (Check segment breakdown)
- Is the competition really catching up? (Check market share data)
- Is the regulatory risk real or noise? (Check precedent, lobbying spend)
- Is management's guidance credible? (Check track record)

## Building the Thesis

A thesis is NOT a price target. It's a **causal argument for why the price should move**.

### The Thesis Formula

> "I believe [ASSET] will [DIRECTION] because [CAUSAL MECHANISM]. The market is currently pricing in [CONSENSUS], but it's missing [KEY VARIABLE]. The catalyst is [WHAT CHANGES THE EQUILIBRIUM]. I would be wrong if [DISCONFIRMING EVENT]."

### What Makes a Strong Thesis

1. **Causal mechanism** — Not "momentum is positive" but "earnings estimates are about to be revised upward because [specific reason]"
2. **Specific edge** — What do you see that the market doesn't? What is mispriced?
3. **Catalyst** — What event or data point will cause the market to re-price?
4. **Falsifiability** — What specific outcome would prove you wrong?
5. **Time-bound** — Over what time horizon should this thesis play out?

### What Makes a Weak Thesis

1. ❌ "The stock looks cheap" — Cheap relative to what? Why is it cheap — is there a reason?
2. ❌ "Technical breakout" — Why did it break out? Is there fundamental support?
3. ❌ "Analyst upgrades" — What do analysts know that you don't? Why now?
4. ❌ "It's going up" — That's an observation, not a thesis
5. ❌ "AI is a big tailwind" — Is that already priced in? What's the edge?

### Examples: Weak vs Strong Thesis

**Weak**: "AAPL is a great company with strong fundamentals, I think it goes up."

**Strong**: "AAPL will outperform over the next 6 months because the iPhone upgrade cycle is being underestimated — install base is at record size, replacement cycle is at 4.1 years (longest ever), and the AI features in iPhone 17 will trigger a super-cycle. The market is pricing in ~2% Services growth deceleration, but App Store revenue is re-accelerating due to AI app subscriptions. Catalyst: Q3 earnings in July will show iPhone unit surprise. I would be wrong if Chinese demand deteriorates further or the AI features fail to drive upgrade intent."

## Common Thesis Mistakes

### 1. Confusing a View with a Thesis

A view: "I like this stock."
A thesis: "This stock will go up because [specific mechanism] and I have edge because [specific mispricing]."

### 2. Thesis Without Edge

If you can't articulate what the market is missing, you don't have a thesis — you have an opinion. And your opinion is already in the price.

### 3. Thesis Without Catalyst

Even if you're right, WHEN does the market realize it? Without a catalyst, you can be right and still lose money. The market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent.

### 4. Unfalsifiable Thesis

"I think it goes up eventually" is not falsifiable. Every thesis needs a kill switch: "If X happens, I'm wrong and I exit."

### 5. Thesis After Data

Looking at all the data first and then constructing a thesis is indistinguishable from confirmation bias. The data should TEST your thesis, not CREATE it.

---

## When You Can't Form a Thesis

Sometimes you genuinely don't have enough information to form a view. That's OK. In that case:

1. **State what you don't know** — "I can't form a thesis because I don't understand [X]"
2. **Identify the research needed** — "I need to understand [X] before I can have a view"
3. **Either research it or pass** — Don't fake confidence when you don't have it

**Honest uncertainty is better than false conviction.**
