# Self-Verification Protocol

## Why Self-Verify

Your first instinct is your worst analysis. The mind:
- Seeks confirming evidence automatically (confirmation bias)
- Constructs narratives from random patterns (narrative fallacy)
- Overweights recent and vivid information (availability/recency bias)
- Anchors to the first number it sees (anchoring bias)

**The purpose of self-verification is not to make you doubt everything — it's to make your surviving conviction genuinely earned.**

---

## The Red-Team Protocol

You MUST complete at least ONE full red-team cycle before stating any recommendation.

### Round 1: State Initial Thesis

Write down your thesis clearly:

```
THESIS: [ASSET] will [DIRECTION] because [CAUSAL MECHANISM]
EDGE: The market is missing [KEY VARIABLE]
CATALYST: [WHAT CHANGES THE EQUILIBRIUM]
KILL SWITCH: I exit if [SPECIFIC EVENT]
```

### Round 2: Steel-Man the Opposition

Build the **strongest possible** case against your thesis. Not a straw man — a steel man.

Answer each of these:

#### 1. What is the best argument for the OPPOSITE of my thesis?

Not a weak counter — the most compelling, intellectually honest counter-argument you can construct. Give it a fair hearing.

**Example**: If you're bullish on NVDA, the steel-man bear case isn't "AI is a fad" (weak). It's: "The capex cycle is front-loaded — hyperscalers are spending $200B in 2 years to build infrastructure that lasts 5+ years. Once the build-out is done, NVDA's data center revenue halves. The market is pricing in perpetual 40% growth, but the upgrade cycle is much smaller than the initial build."

#### 2. What if the market ALREADY KNOWS what I know?

This is the most dangerous question. If your "edge" is something everyone already knows (e.g., "AI is growing fast"), it's already in the price. The market is a discounting machine — it prices KNOWN information.

**Test**: Is this insight in the last 3 analyst reports? Is it on the front page of Seeking Alpha? If yes, it's probably priced in.

#### 3. What is the WEAKEST link in my causal chain?

Your thesis has a causal chain: A → B → C → price moves. Which link is the weakest? Where is the causal logic most uncertain?

**Example**: "AI adoption grows → companies buy more GPUs → NVDA revenue grows → stock goes up." The weakest link might be: "Do companies actually BUY more GPUs, or do they rent cloud AI services (which amortizes GPU spend across many customers)?"

#### 4. What am I assuming that might not be true?

List your implicit assumptions. Which ones are you most uncertain about?

- "I'm assuming margins hold" → Will competition erode them?
- "I'm assuming the Fed doesn't hike again" → What if inflation re-accelerates?
- "I'm assuming the moat is durable" → Has it been tested by a real competitor?
- "I'm assuming management executes" → What's their track record?

#### 5. Who is on the OTHER SIDE of this trade?

Every trade has a counterparty. Who are they, and why are they wrong?

- If you're buying, someone is selling. Why are THEY selling?
- If you're short, someone is buying. What do THEY see?
- Are they informed (insiders, institutions) or uninformed (retail momentum)?

#### 6. What would a SMART PERSON who disagrees say?

Channel someone who knows this asset well and has the opposite view. What would they say? Take it seriously.

---

### Round 3: Targeted Research to Adjudicate

After building the opposing case, identify the **1-2 facts that would resolve the disagreement**.

- If the disagreement is about growth sustainability → Find forward order book, customer pipeline, or capex plans
- If the disagreement is about competitive dynamics → Find market share data, pricing trends, new entrant info
- If the disagreement is about regulatory risk → Find precedent, lobbying spend, regulatory body statements
- If the disagreement is about management credibility → Find historical guidance vs actual results

**Then research those specific facts.** Don't just think harder — find the data that resolves the uncertainty.

---

### Round 4: Revise and Re-rate

After the red-team cycle, explicitly compare:

```
BEFORE RED-TEAM:
- Thesis: [original]
- Conviction: X/10
- Key assumption: [what you assumed]

AFTER RED-TEAM:
- Thesis: [revised — did it change?]
- Conviction: X/10 [usually lower — that's healthy]
- Key assumption: [revised — what did you learn?]
- New unknown: [what the red team revealed that you didn't know before]
```

**It is NOT a failure if your conviction drops.** A thesis that survives challenge at 7/10 conviction is worth far more than an untested thesis at 9/10.

## The Confidence Kill Switches

These conditions should trigger an automatic confidence downgrade or a PASS:

### Immediate PASS (Don't Trade)
- You can't articulate a causal mechanism (only pattern/indicator)
- You can't identify what the market is missing (no edge)
- You can't state what would make you wrong (unfalsifiable)
- Expected value < friction costs (no profitable trade possible)
- The opposing case is STRONGER than your thesis

### Downgrade to LOW conviction
- The red-team changed your thesis significantly
- Your weakest causal link is genuinely uncertain
- The "edge" might already be priced in
- Key data is missing or unreliable

### Downgrade from HIGH to MEDIUM
- The opposing case is plausible but not compelling
- One assumption is uncertain, the rest are solid
- Catalyst timing is unclear

### Maintain HIGH conviction (rare — earn this)
- Your thesis survived the red team WITHOUT significant revision
- The opposing case has a clear factual error
- You have genuine information edge (the market is demonstrably mispricing something)
- Catalyst is clear and near-term
- Risk is defined and manageable

---

## Iterative Self-Improvement

The red-team protocol is not a one-time checklist. If your thesis changes significantly after the red team, you should consider running a SECOND red-team on the revised thesis.

**Why?** Because your revised thesis might have new weaknesses that you haven't examined yet.

**Practical rule**: If the red team changes your recommendation (BUY → HOLD, HOLD → SELL, etc.), do at least one more verification round on the new recommendation.

---

## Pre-Output Quality Check

Before delivering your analysis, verify:

- [ ] Did I build the strongest opposing case I could? (Not just the easiest to knock down?)
- [ ] Did I identify my weakest evidence and address it?
- [ ] Did I check whether my edge might be priced in?
- [ ] Did I research the specific facts that would resolve the disagreement?
- [ ] Did I revise my conviction based on what I learned?
- [ ] Can I state, in one sentence, what would change my mind?
- [ ] Is my recommendation the same AFTER verification as before? (If not, that's good — you grew.)

**A thesis that was never tested is not a thesis. It's a hope.**
