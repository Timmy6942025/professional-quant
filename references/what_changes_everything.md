# What Changes Everything

## The One-Fact Framework

In most analyses, 2-4 variables drive 80%+ of the outcome. But among those, there is often ONE variable that, if it changed, would flip the entire thesis. Finding that variable — or the fact about it that the market doesn't appreciate — is the highest-value research you can do.

**Most analysis is noise. The edge is in finding the signal that changes the decision.**

---

## Categories of "Everything-Changers"

### 1. Margin Inflection

When margins change direction, everything changes. A 2% margin expansion on a 10% base margin DOUBLES earnings growth if revenue is flat.

**What to look for**:
- Operating leverage: Are fixed costs about to be spread over higher revenue?
- Input cost reversal: Are commodity inputs about to drop?
- Pricing power test: Did the company just raise prices without losing customers?
- Mix shift: Is the higher-margin segment growing faster?

**Example**: "The market models TSMC at 38% gross margin. But their N3 process node is yielding at 80%+ — up from 50% at launch. As N3 mix increases from 15% to 30% of revenue, blended gross margin expands to 42%. That's a 400bps margin surprise worth ~$8B in incremental earnings."

### 2. Regulatory Ruling

One ruling can restructure an industry's economics overnight.

**What to look for**:
- Pending legislation or regulatory decisions
- Court cases with industry-wide implications
- Policy shifts that create or destroy moats
- International regulatory divergence (e.g., EU vs US on tech regulation)

**Example**: "The market prices LLY at 35x earnings assuming continued GLP-1 pricing power. But the Inflation Reduction Act's Medicare negotiation provisions apply to Mounjaro starting 2027. If the negotiated price is 40-50% below list, that's a $5-8B revenue headwind the market hasn't fully modeled."

### 3. Management Credibility Shift

When the market stops believing management, the discount rate expands permanently. When it starts believing, multiples re-rate.

**What to look for**:
- Has management consistently beaten or missed guidance?
- Are they overpromising and underdelivering, or the reverse?
- Has there been a CEO/CFO change that could reset credibility?
- Are insiders buying or selling?

**Example**: "META's Zuckerberg spent $36B on metaverse with no revenue to show. The market gave him a 10x multiple. Then he pivoted to 'Year of Efficiency,' cut costs, and proved capital discipline. The multiple re-rated from 10x to 25x in 18 months. The only thing that changed was management credibility on capital allocation."

### 4. Competitive Dynamic Shift

When the competitive landscape shifts, margins, growth, and multiple all change.

**What to look for**:
- Is a major competitor exiting, failing, or being acquired?
- Is a new entrant with a fundamentally different cost structure emerging?
- Are industry margins expanding (under-invested) or compressing (over-competitive)?
- Is the market structure shifting from fragmented to concentrated (or vice versa)?

**Example**: "Intel's manufacturing struggles opened a 3-year window for TSMC and AMD to gain share. By the time Intel recovers, AMD will have locked in enterprise x86 market share gains of 5-8 points. The market models AMD's share as cyclical; it's actually structural."

### 5. Refinancing Wall

When debt needs to be refinanced at much higher rates, the math changes dramatically.

**What to look for**:
- Maturity walls: When does significant debt come due?
- Current coupon vs. likely refinancing rate
- Covenant headroom: How close are they to breaching covenants?
- Is there a rising-rate environment making refinancing expensive?

**Example**: "This REIT has $2B of debt maturing in 2026 at an average 3.5% coupon. Refinancing at current rates (6.5%) adds $60M/year in interest expense — that's 15% of current FFO. The market prices the stock at current FFO yield, but post-refinancing FFO will be meaningfully lower."

### 6. Customer Concentration / Dependency

When one customer matters too much, the entire investment thesis rests on that relationship.

**What to look for**:
- Top 1 customer as % of revenue
- Top 5 customers as % of revenue
- Is concentration increasing or decreasing?
- What happens if the largest customer cuts orders by 20%?

**Example**: "This semiconductor equipment company does 35% of revenue with Samsung. Samsung just announced a capex cut for memory. Even if other customers grow, a 20% Samsung order reduction = -7% revenue decline. The market models 5% growth."

### 7. Supply Shock or Structural Supply Change

When supply conditions change, the cost structure and competitive dynamics can shift permanently.

**What to look for**:
- Is there a geographic concentration of supply that could be disrupted?
- Are input costs about to change structurally (not cyclically)?
- Is there a technological change that could reduce input requirements?
- Are supply chains being reshored/nearshored, changing cost structure?

### 8. Narrative Shift

When the market's STORY about an asset changes, the multiple changes even if the fundamentals haven't.

**What to look for**:
- Has the media narrative shifted from bearish to bullish (or vice versa)?
- Has the consensus view of the company's TAM changed?
- Has the company been reclassified in investors' minds? (Value → Growth, Cyclical → Secular)
- Is a new narrative forming that could drive multiple expansion/contraction?

**Example**: "TSLA was priced as a car company (10x earnings) until 2020. Then the narrative shifted to 'energy + AI + robotics company' and the multiple expanded to 80x+. The fundamentals didn't change 8x — the narrative did."

## How to Find the "Everything-Changer"

### The Backward Method

Start from the current valuation and work backwards:

1. "What does the current price ASSUME?" (growth rate, margins, multiple)
2. "Is that assumption reasonable?" (check against history, peers, common sense)
3. "What would BREAK that assumption?" (find the everything-changer)
4. "Is that break likely?" (assess probability)

### The Forward Method

Start from the business and work forward:

1. "What are the 3 most important variables for this business?"
2. "For each variable, what would constitute a SURPRISE?" (vs consensus expectations)
3. "How likely is each surprise?" (base rates, history, structural analysis)
4. "If the surprise happens, how much does the thesis change?"

### The Contrarian Method

Start from the consensus and look for what it's missing:

1. "What does EVERYONE believe about this asset?"
2. "What would have to be true for everyone to be WRONG?"
3. "Is there any evidence that this wrong-case is actually happening?"
4. "If the consensus is wrong, how big is the re-pricing?"

## The "What If" Stress Test

For every analysis, explicitly run these stress tests:

| Stress Test | Question | If True, Impact |
|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Margin collapse | What if margins revert to industry average? | Usually -30-50% on earnings |
| Multiple contraction | What if the multiple reverts to historical average? | Usually -20-40% on price |
| Key customer loss | What if the top customer leaves? | Revenue shock, margin shock |
| Regulatory change | What if the most feared regulation passes? | Business model restructuring |
| Management failure | What if management's plan fails? | Multiple compression, earnings miss |
| Macro regime change | What if we enter a recession? | Cyclical earnings decline |
| Competitive entry | What if a well-funded competitor enters? | Margin compression, share loss |
| Refinancing shock | What if rates spike when debt matures? | Interest expense doubling |

**If any of these would DESTROY your thesis and you haven't assessed its probability, you're not done analyzing.**

---

## The Decisive Question

At the end of every analysis, answer this one question:

> **"If I could know ONE thing about this asset that I don't currently know, what would it be?"**

That's the variable that matters most. That's what your research should have focused on. If you didn't answer it — your analysis has a gap.

**The best analysts don't know everything. They know what matters.**
