# Cognitive Biases & Second-Level Thinking

> **Role in this skill**: Supplementary reference — a detailed bias catalog and pre-trade checklist. Use as a *lookup* when you detect bias in your reasoning, not as a mandatory worksheet. For the active bias-combatting protocol, see `references/self_verification.md` and `references/model_pitfalls.md`.

## Biases That Destroy Returns

### 1. Confirmation Bias
- **What it is**: Seeking info that confirms existing beliefs
- **In trading**: Only looking at bullish indicators when you want to BUY
- **Danger**: Ignores warning signs that should change your mind
- **Fix**: Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Ask "What would prove me WRONG?"

### 2. Recency Bias
- **What it is**: Overweighting recent events
- **In trading**: Thinking crash is imminent after 3 red days
- **Danger**: Causes premature exits and entries based on short-term noise
- **Fix**: Always compare to 3-month, 6-month, 1-year context. Use longer timeframes.

### 3. Anchoring Bias
- **What it is**: Fixating on arbitrary reference points
- **In trading**: "I bought at $100, so it's cheap at $90" (price doesn\'t care about your entry)
- **Danger**: Holding losers, avoiding profitable positions
- **Fix**: Price is where it is. Ask "Would I buy TODAY at current price?"

### 4. Loss Aversion
- **What it is**: Feeling losses 2x more than gains (Prospect Theory)
- **In trading**: Holding losers too long, selling winners too early
- **Danger**: Asymmetric returns - small wins, big losses
- **Fix**: Have RULES. Cut losers at -8%, let winners run to +20%.

### 5. Herding Bias
- **What it is**: Following the crowd
- **In trading**: Buying because "everyone loves tech stocks"
- **Danger**: Buy at tops, sell at bottoms
- **Fix**: Be contrarian when sentiment is extreme. Buy fear, sell greed.

### 6. Overconfidence Bias
- **What it is**: Overestimating prediction ability
- **In trading**: "I KNOW it's going to $300"
- **Danger**: Oversized positions, ignored risk management
- **Fix**: Use probabilities, not certainties. "70% chance" not "it will go up"

### 7. Availability Bias
- **What it is**: Overestimating memorable events
- **In trading**: Fearful after 2008 crash, even in 2024 bull market
- **Danger**: Misses opportunities in "boring" uptrends
- **Fix**: Use base rates. What % of time does this pattern actually work?

### 8. Sunk Cost Fallacy
- **What it is**: Continuing because you\'ve "invested so much already"
- **In trading**: Holding bag because "I can\'t sell at a loss"
- **Danger**: Good money follows bad money into losing positions
- **Fix**: Every day, ask "Would I buy this TODAY at current price?" If no, sell.

### 9. Survivorship Bias
- **What it is**: Only seeing winners, not losers
- **In trading**: Looking at AMZN/TSLA success, ignoring 1000s of failed companies
- **Danger**: Unrealistic expectations, underestimating failure rates
- **Fix**: Consider what you DON\'T see. For every success story, how many failures?

### 10. Narrative Fallacy
- **What it is**: Creating stories to explain random events
- **In trading**: "Market dropped because of tweet" (when it was just normal volatility)
- **Danger**: False confidence in causal relationships
- **Fix**: Separate noise from signal. Most daily moves are random.

### 11. The Dunning-Kruger Effect
- **What it is**: Low ability people overestimate their competence
- **In trading**: Beginner thinks they\'ve figured out the market in 3 months
- **Danger**: Overtrading, overleveraging, catastrophic losses
- **Fix**: Realize how much you don\'t know. Read, study, practice for YEARS.

### 12. Disposition Effect
- **What it is**: Sell winners too early, hold losers too long
- **In trading**: Taking profit at +5% while letting losses run to -20%
- **Danger**: Reverses the optimal pattern (cut winners, let losers run)
- **Fix**: Use hard stops. Trail stops on winners. Never average into losers.

### 13. Hindsight Bias
- **What it is**: Seeing past events as predictable
- **In trading**: "I KNEW that would happen" after it does
- **Danger**: Overconfidence in predictions
- **Fix**: Keep a trading journal. Record predictions BEFORE outcomes.

### 14. Framing Effect
- **What it is**: Same info framed differently changes decisions
- **In trading**: "80% win rate" sounds better than "20% lose rate"
- **Danger**: Manipulated by how information is presented
- **Fix**: Always look at both sides of framing. Understand true odds.

### 15. Endowment Effect
- **What it is**: Valuing what you own more than market value
- **In trading**: "My stock is worth more than it is" because I own it
- **Danger**: Overvaluing positions, missing better opportunities
- **Fix**: Pretend you don\'t own it. Would you buy it today at market price?

## Second-Level Thinking (Howard Marks Style)

### First-Level Thinking (Weak)
- "It's a good company, stock will go up"
- "Bad earnings, stock will go down"
- "Everyone is bullish, I should buy"
- **Problem**: Surface-level, everyone thinks this, already priced in

### Second-Level Thinking (Strong)
- "It's a good company, but everyone knows that and it's overvalued at 50x P/E"
- "Bad earnings, but guidance was raised and stock is down 20% on bad sentiment - BUY"
- "Everyone is bullish and positioned long, so who is left to buy? Time to sell."
- **Benefit**: Contrarian, probabilistic, considers what\'s PRICED IN

### The Second-Level Thinking Test
Ask yourself:
1. What is the consensus view?
2. What is already priced in?
3. What would make consensus WRONG?
4. What would make consensus RIGHT but still not profitable?
5. What am I seeing that others are missing?
6. What does my position depend on that isn\'t certain?

## The Professional Trader Mindset

What separates legends from average traders:

### 1. They Embrace Uncertainty
- Not: "I know what will happen"
- Instead: "I don\'t know, but here\'s 60% probability"
- This allows for proper position sizing and risk management

### 2. They Think in Expected Value
```
Not: "This will make me money"
Instead: "This trade has 60% chance of +10% and 40% chance of -5%
         EV = (0.6 × 10) - (0.4 × 5) = 6 - 2 = +4%"
```

### 3. They\'re Process-Focused
- Not: "Did I make money?"
- Instead: "Did I follow my system? Did I manage risk?"
- Good process can have bad outcome. Bad process can have good outcome.
- Focus on process, results will follow.

### 4. They\'re Probabilistic
- Not: "It will go up"
- Instead: "Likely to go up (65% probability), target $XXX, stop $XXX"
- NeverCertainty, always probability

### 5. They\'re Self-Aware
- Not: "I\'m bullish because my analysis is good"
- Instead: "I\'m bullish because I WANT to be, let me check my biases"
- Actively question your own motives and cognitive biases

### 6. They Know What They Don\'t Know
- Not: "I understand this stock completely"
- Instead: "I understand X, Y, Z but I\'m uncertain about A, B, C"
- Humility is a competitive advantage in markets

## Pre-Trade Bias Checklist

Before ANY trade, go through this checklist:

### Confirmation & Anchoring
- [ ] Am I seeking only bullish information? (Seek bearish too)
- [ ] Am I anchoring to my entry price? (Price doesn\'t care)
- [ ] Would I buy this TODAY at current price if I didn\'t own it?

### Recency & Availability
- [ ] Am I overweighting the last 3-5 days? (Check longer timeframe)
- [ ] Am I influenced by a memorable event? (Check base rates)
- [ ] Is this trade based on recent news or longer-term analysis?

### Herding & Social
- [ ] Am I following the crowd? (Check contrarian view)
- [ ] Is sentiment extreme? (Opposite of extreme = opportunity)
- [ ] Who am I copying and why?

### Overconfidence
- [ ] Am I certain this will work? (Replace with probability)
- [ ] Is position size appropriate for conviction? (High conviction = larger size)
- [ ] What would make me WRONG? (Define exit before entry)

### Expected Value
- [ ] What is the win probability? (Based on data, not hope)
- [ ] What is the average win size?
- [ ] What is the average loss size?
- [ ] Is EV positive? (Win% × Win - Loss% × Loss > 0)

### Process vs Outcome
- [ ] Does this fit my trading system?
- [ ] Is risk:reward at least 1:2?
- [ ] Do I have an entry, stop, and target?
- [ ] Am I breaking a rule? (If yes, don\'t trade)

## Common Bias Mistakes & Fixes

| Bias | Mistake | Fix |
|------|---------|-----|
| Confirmation | Only read bullish articles | Actively seek bearish perspectives |
| Recency | Sold after 3 red days | Check longer timeframe trend |
| Anchoring | "It\'ll come back to my entry" | Pretend you\'re new, buy or sell based on today\'s price |
| Loss Aversion | Sold winners at +5% | Trail stops, let winners run |
| Herding | Bought at top following crowd | Be contrarian at extremes |
| Overconfidence | 50% position on "sure thing" | Use probability-based sizing |
| Sunk Cost | Held losing position | Daily "would I buy today?" test |
| Narrative | Created story for random move | Focus on data, not stories |

## The Ultimate Self-Check

Before hitting "buy" or "sell", ask yourself:

1. **Am I calm or emotional?** (If emotional, don\'t trade)
2. **Am I following my system?** (If no, don\'t trade)
3. **Do I have an edge?** (Why will this work?)
4. **What\'s my "out"?** (If price hits X, I\'m wrong)
5. **Is position size appropriate?** (Risk < 2% of portfolio per trade)
6. **What\'s the base rate?** (How often does this pattern work?)
7. **Am I being contrarian or following the herd?**
8. **What am I not seeing?** (Unknown unknowns)

If you can\'t answer these clearly, WAIT. The market will be there tomorrow.
