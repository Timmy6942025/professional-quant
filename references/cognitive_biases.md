# Cognitive Biases in Trading

## Biases That Destroy Returns

### Confirmation Bias
- **What it is**: Seeking info that confirms existing beliefs
- **In trading**: Only looking at bullish indicators when you want to BUY
- **Fix**: Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Play devil's advocate.

### Recency Bias
- **What it is**: Overweighting recent events
- **In trading**: Thinking crash is imminent after 3 red days
- **Fix**: Always compare to 3-month, 6-month, 1-year context

### Anchoring
- **What it is**: Fixating on arbitrary reference points
- **In trading**: "I bought at $100, so it's cheap at $90"
- **Fix**: Price is where it is. Fundamentals + technicals matter, not your entry.

### Loss Aversion
- **What it is**: Feeling losses 2x more than gains
- **In trading**: Holding losers too long, selling winners too early
- **Fix**: Have rules. Cut losers at -8%, let winners run to +15%.

### Herding Bias
- **What it is**: Following the crowd
- **In trading**: Buying because "everyone loves tech stocks"
- **Fix**: Be contrarian when sentiment is extreme. Buy fear, sell greed.

### Overconfidence
- **What it is**: Overestimating prediction ability
- **In trading**: "I KNOW it's going to $300"
- **Fix**: Use probabilities, not certainties. "70% chance of upmove" not "It will go up".

### Availability Bias
- **What it is**: Overestimating memorable events
- **In trading**: Fearful after 2008 crash, even in 2024 bull market
- **Fix**: Use base rates. What % of the time does this pattern actually work?

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
- **What it is**: Continuing because you've "invested so much already"
- **In trading**: Holding bag because "I can't sell at a loss"
- **Fix**: Every day, ask "Would I buy this TODAY at current price?" If no, sell.

### Survivorship Bias
- **What it is**: Only seeing winners, not losers
- **In trading**: Looking at AMZN/TSLA success, ignoring 1000s of failed companies
- **Fix**: Consider what you DON'T see. For every success story, how many failures?

### Narrative Fallacy
- **What it is**: Creating stories to explain random events
- **In trading**: "Market dropped because of tweet" (when it was just normal volatility)
- **Fix**: Separate noise from signal. Most daily moves are random.

## Second-Level Thinking (Howard Marks)

### First-Level Thinking
- "It's a good company, stock will go up"
- "Bad earnings, stock will go down"
- Surface-level, everyone thinks this

### Second-Level Thinking
- "It's a good company, but everyone knows that and it's overvalued at 50x P/E"
- "Bad earnings, but guidance was raised and stock is down 20% on bad sentiment - BUY"
- Contrarian, probabilistic, considers what's PRICED IN

## Checklist to Avoid Biases

Before any trade, verify:
- [ ] Did I seek disconfirming evidence?
- [ ] Am I anchoring to my entry price?
- [ ] Is recency bias affecting me (last 3 days)?
- [ ] Would I buy this TODAY at current price (ignoring my position)?
- [ ] Am I following the herd?
- [ ] Is my conviction %-based or absolute?
- [ ] What's the base rate for this pattern?
- [ ] What story am I telling myself that might be wrong?

## The "Insane" Mindset

Insane traders think differently:
1. **They embrace uncertainty** - "I don't know, but here's 60% probability"
2. **They think in expected value** - (Win % × Win Size) - (Loss % × Loss Size)
3. **They're process-focused** - "Good process can have bad outcome, bad process can have good outcome"
4. **They're probabilistic** - Never "will", always "likely to"
5. **They're self-aware** - "I'm bullish because I want to be, let me check my biases"
